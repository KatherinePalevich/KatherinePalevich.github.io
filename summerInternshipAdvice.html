Guidelines for a successful 12-week tech internship

Hello! My name is Katherine. I interned at Pinterest in the summer of 2025 and at Experian in the summer of 2024. [cite: 1] This timeline is based on my experience at large tech companies as an iOS engineer, where projects typically run 4-8 weeks. [cite: 2] Coming from the University of Washington, which uses a quarter system, I often joined later cohorts in June. [cite: 3, 4] Please treat these checkpoints as guidelines for your own journey. [cite: 5, 6, 7]


<Week 1: Ease into it>

Focus on setting up your environment and building a foundation. [cite: 9]

  • Set up corporate, developer, and insurance accounts; save passwords in a personal account for future access. [cite: 10]
  • Research company perks (like employee wellness funds) and check contribution timelines for HSAs or 401ks. [cite: 10, 11, 13, 14]
  • Start a journal to track tasks, feedback, and "screenshot wins" for your performance reviews. [cite: 16, 17, 18, 20]
  • Exhaust documentation and internal search tools before asking your mentor for help on your ramp-up project. [cite: 23, 24, 25]
  • Socialize with other interns and your team; ask your manager about the intern rubric and expectations for a return offer. [cite: 27, 35, 41, 43, 50, 54]

<Week 2: Defining Workflow>

Establish communication patterns and identify key resources. [cite: 62]

  • Align with your mentor on daily vs. weekly updates and ask which recurring meetings are actually worth your time. [cite: 63, 64, 69, 72]
  • Aim to finish your ramp-up project by the end of this week. [cite: 74]
  • Schedule coffee chats (max 2x a week) with PMs, designers, and engineers to understand the team history. [cite: 42, 75, 76]
  • Join Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) to find community. [cite: 79]

<Week 3: The Big Project Begins>

  • Start your first major project. [cite: 81]
  • Talk to interns in earlier cohorts about their mid-internship reviews to learn from their feedback early. [cite: 82, 83, 85]

<Week 4: Visibility>

  • Volunteer to demo your progress in team meetings to practice showcasing your work. [cite: 88, 90]
  • Immediately correct any negative habits identified in previous feedback loops. [cite: 87]

<Week 5 & 6: Mid-Internship Review>

This is a critical pivot point for growth. [cite: 94, 96]

  • Review your journal for technical and interpersonal wins; use AI to polish your self-review writing. [cite: 97, 104]
  • If your grade is lower than expected, don't worry—managers want to see improvement by the end. [cite: 106, 107, 108]
  • If you don't receive concrete action items for improvement, explicitly ask for them. [cite: 110, 113]
  • Wrap up your first project and transition to the second. [cite: 117]

<Week 7 & 8: Broadening Perspectives>

  • Meet your skip-manager to discuss their career journey. [cite: 119, 121]
  • Attend the first cohort's project showcases; take notes on what makes their presentations engaging (graphics, demos, etc.). [cite: 125, 126, 127]

<Week 9 & 10: The Home Stretch>

  • Push hard to complete remaining project tasks. [cite: 130, 132]
  • Identify any remaining people in the company you want to meet and schedule those chats. [cite: 134, 135]

<Week 11: Polish & Perspective>

  • Meet with directors or marketing leads to see how your SWE work fits into the broader business. [cite: 137, 138]
  • Start your final presentation; record a 3-minute version for practice before the full 15-minute org presentation. [cite: 140, 143, 144]

<Week 12: Wrapping Up>

Leave a lasting positive impression. [cite: 147]

  • Present your final work and create hand-off documentation for the next engineer. [cite: 149, 150]
  • Build or update "ramp-up docs" to help future interns. [cite: 152, 153]
  • Reiterate your desire for a return offer to your manager. [cite: 159]
  • Request LinkedIn recommendations and save personal contact info for your network. [cite: 161, 163, 164]

<Last Day: Savor the Moment>

Send a thank-you message to the Slack org, take photos of your workspace (no sensitive info!), and turn in your gear. [cite: 167, 168, 169, 170] Congratulations on finishing! [cite: 172]
