</Advice For Summer Internships>

Hello! My name is Katherine, I interned at Pinterest in the summer of 2025 and at Experian in the summer of 2024. This experience is heavily biased towards working at a large tech company, and as an iOS engineer where projects are usually 4 - 8 weeks long. I attended the University of Washington which is on a quarter system, meaning I was part of a later cohort starting in early June. Use these checkpoints as guidelines for your progress!


Week 1: Ease into it

  • Carefully set up your accounts and keep track of passwords in a specific document. Save usernames and passwords in a personal account for any accounts you may need access to after leaving.
  • Set up HSA, 401k, and Roth IRA contribution amounts as they become available.
  • Learn what company perks you get and spend your employee wellness funds before the quarter ends.
  • Set up a journal to keep track of tasks, meeting notes, and feedback. This is your "receipt" for performance reviews.
  • Note who you talked to and save useful links. Create a tab for screenshots of praise messages for a morale boost.
  • Exhaust documentation and internal search tools before asking humans for help. When asking, provide context on what you already tried.
  • Get lunch with other interns and team members. Meet your manager to discuss expectations, the performance rubric, and your goals for a return offer.

Week 2: Defining Communication

  • Establish communication expectations with your mentor—daily vs. weekly syncs.
  • Ask for knowledge resources and identify which recurring meetings are actually relevant to your project.
  • Summarize meeting points and action items for your mentor to show progress.
  • Aim to finish your ramp-up project by the end of this week.
  • Set up coffee chats (max twice a week) with PMs, UX designers, and engineers of various levels.

Week 3 & 4: Projects and Demos

  • Begin your first big project.
  • Talk to interns in earlier cohorts about their mid-internship reviews to learn from their feedback early.
  • Volunteer to demo your work in team meetings to practice showcasing your progress.

Week 5 & 6: Mid-Internship Review

  • Review your journal for technical and interpersonal wins. Align these with the intern rubric keywords.
  • Use your review to identify concrete action items for improvement. Don't assume everything is fine; always look for ways to grow.
  • Wrap up your first project and start your second.

Week 7 & 8: Skip-Levels and Showcases

  • Meet your skip-manager to learn about their career journey and get high-level business context.
  • Attend final intern presentations for the first cohort. Take notes on what makes a presentation effective—graphics, demos, and high-level technical summaries.

Week 11 & 12: Wrapping Up

  • Start your end-of-internship presentation early. Record a short practice video and a longer technical version for your org.
  • Create hand-off documents and update internal ramp-up docs to help the next hire.
  • Set up final meetings with your mentor and manager. Reiterate your desire for a return offer and request LinkedIn recommendations.

Last Day

Send out thank-you messages, take pictures to remember the experience (no sensitive info!), and turn in your equipment. Congratulations on successfully completing your summer internship!
